## "Just checking in" is the annoying part.

Founders think follow-ups are annoying because they're bothering the investor. Wrong. Follow-ups are annoying when they deliver nothing — when the email is a groveling "just wanted to bump this to the top of your inbox" with no new information.

Investors don't mind follow-ups that carry signal. They mind follow-ups that ask for their time without giving them anything back.

Every follow-up needs a reason: new proof point, specific next step, structural update, deadline. If you don't have a reason, don't send the email. Wait until you do.

## The right cadence, by stage

Different stages of the conversation need different follow-up rhythms:

### After initial outreach — 48 hours

If you cold-emailed a VC and haven't heard back in 48 hours, one follow-up is fine. Keep it under 3 sentences.

Weak follow-up:
> "Hi [Name], just wanted to bump this in case it got buried. Let me know if you'd like to chat."

Strong follow-up:
> "Following up on the pre-seed intro for [Company] — closed 3 more angel commitments this week, now 60% of the way to target. Deck attached, happy to send investor brief if useful."

The strong version delivers new information (angels closed, momentum signal). The weak version is empty.

If they still don't respond after the 48-hour follow-up, move on. Two follow-ups on a cold intro is the max.

### After a first meeting — within 24 hours

Send within 24 hours. This isn't a follow-up — it's the follow-through from the meeting.

Standard structure:
- Thank you for the time
- One-sentence recap of what you discussed
- Specific items you're delivering (financials, references, product access)
- Specific next step you're proposing

Example:
> "Thanks for the time yesterday, [Name]. Appreciated the questions on retention. Attaching the cohort curves we discussed + the customer reference sheet. Would a second meeting the week of [date] work to walk through the fundraising ask in detail?"

That's a follow-up that carries value. Investor knows you're organized, you're delivering what you promised, and you have a specific next step in mind.

### After they say "let me discuss with the partnership" — 5 business days

VCs use "let me discuss with the partnership" to mean many things. Sometimes it's a real next step. Often it's a soft pass. Five business days is the right window to check in.

At 5 business days:
> "Following up on [Company] — wanted to check where things landed after the partnership discussion. Also worth flagging: we closed a $250K commitment from [Named Angel] this week, so the round is picking up momentum."

New information + specific check on their internal process. Not groveling.

If they don't respond within 3-4 days of that follow-up, it's a soft pass. See [How do I know if a VC passed](/raise-intel/how-do-i-know-if-a-vc-passed-or-is-just-slow).

### During diligence — weekly

Once you're in active due diligence, weekly check-ins with new proof are normal. Cadence:

Every Monday or Tuesday, send:
- One new proof point (new customer, new metric, new hire, new milestone)
- Any specific DD items you're delivering that week
- Any specific asks (references you need scheduled, questions you need answered)

Example:
> "Weekly update [Company] — signed [Named Customer] to a paid pilot last Friday, $8K ARR. Sending updated cohort data + churn analysis this afternoon. Also, if we can get [Reference X and Y] scheduled for next week that would keep us on the target close date."

New info + delivery + specific ask. Every week during DD.

### For "let me stay in touch" investors — monthly

Some investors soft-pass with "would love to stay in touch as you make progress." A small portion of these actually come back at seed or Series A when they see traction. Most don't.

Monthly touch cadence:
- Send a short update the first Tuesday of each month
- 3-5 sentences maximum
- Actual progress: revenue, hires, customers, product milestones
- No ask

Example:
> "March update for [Company] — MRR at $18K (up from $12K last month), signed [Named Customer], hired Head of Sales. Team of 6 now. Let me know if you'd like to reconnect anytime."

Zero ask. Just information. If they want to reconnect they will.

Investors respect this cadence because it doesn't waste their time. They can skim in 10 seconds and either engage or not.

## What makes follow-up annoying

Common patterns to avoid:

- **Multiple follow-ups on the same thread with no new information.** Sending "just checking in" three times in two weeks reads as desperate.
- **Follow-up + cc'ing the partner's associate + the associate's associate.** Escalation looks amateur. Talk to your primary contact.
- **Follow-up with implicit guilt-tripping.** "I know you're busy, but I wanted to make sure this didn't get lost" — you're begging for attention.
- **Follow-up with fake deadlines.** "We're closing this week, wanted to give you last chance" when you're not actually closing this week. VCs pattern-match to fake urgency instantly.
- **Follow-up asking for their opinion on random tangents.** "Would love your thoughts on our seed vs. Series A framing" — you're asking them to do free advisory work with no commitment.
- **Follow-up over multiple channels (email + LinkedIn + Twitter DM).** One channel per follow-up. If email isn't working, add LinkedIn ONE time. Not both simultaneously.

## The "no reply" default rule

If you sent a follow-up and got no reply within 3-4 business days after the initial no-reply window, treat that as a signal:

- One-off cold outreach + no follow-up reply = they weren't interested, move on
- Post-meeting follow-up + no reply = soft pass, downgrade in pipeline
- DD follow-up + no reply = process breaking, one more check-in then treat as cooling
- "Stay in touch" investor missing a monthly update reply = normal, don't over-read it

Don't chase silence with more follow-ups. Silence is a message.

## What to skip

Follow-up mistakes founders make:

- **Sending an email + calling within an hour to make sure they got it.** Never. One channel.
- **Sending a "cadence" without new information just because the calendar says it's time.** No new info = don't send.
- **Following up on Christmas / New Year / Thanksgiving weeks.** Nobody's reading. Wait until they're back.
- **Following up when you don't have a decision to force.** Some founders follow up expecting a decision when the ball is still in their court. Ask yourself: is there a decision they can make right now with the info I have? If no, don't send.
- **Cc'ing your co-founder or associate on every follow-up.** Cc chains look overly bureaucratic. Keep it 1:1 unless there's a specific reason.

## The specific decision to force with a follow-up

Every follow-up should ideally force ONE of these decisions:

- Book the next meeting?
- Move to reference calls?
- Confirm you're moving to the partner meeting or not?
- Provide the specific DD item you asked for?
- Confirm you're still active in the process or passing?
- Send the term sheet or clarify what's holding it back?

If your follow-up doesn't force one of these, you're just adding noise. Wait until you can.

## FAQ

### How many times can I follow up on cold outreach before it's annoying?

One follow-up (48 hours after original) is expected. Two follow-ups is the max. Three or more = annoying.

### What if my primary contact leaves the firm mid-process?

Reach out to the partner or another associate you interacted with. Don't just wait. VCs expect founders to keep momentum.

### Should I follow up during the holidays?

No. Wait until they're back. Sending on December 24 is worse than not sending.

### What if I don't have new information but the deadline is real?

Then the deadline itself is the information. "Following up — [investor lead] is signing this week and we need decisions by Friday to close within your check size." Real deadlines get respect.

### Is LinkedIn DM appropriate for follow-up?

Sometimes, after email hasn't worked. Once. Not as first-choice. Email is still primary.

### What about following up if I got a formal pass?

Only if you have significantly new proof — 2x revenue, tier-1 lead investor, etc. Otherwise, respect the pass and reconnect at next stage.

### Can I follow up on a follow-up?

Rarely, and only with new information. "Bumping in case buried" is annoying. "Wanted to flag we closed [X named investor] since I emailed" is fine.
