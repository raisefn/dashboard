## Angels first, VCs second. Not one or the other.

Every founder frames this as a choice: "Should I raise $500K from angels or go to VCs for $2M?" That's the wrong question. In almost every case, you want BOTH — and specifically in this order: angels first, VCs second.

The angel round is your cheap validation layer. Angels write small checks, decide fast, and their reasoning matters less than their yes. Ten angel commitments at $25-100K each signals to VCs that the story is real. Then VCs commit at scale.

Skipping angels and going straight to VCs means walking into partner meetings with zero validation. You spend 8-12 weeks building momentum in cold rooms. Skipping VCs and only raising angels caps your round at $500K-$1M — enough for 4-6 months of runway, not enough to prove out the seed milestone.

Both. Angels first. Then VCs.

## Why angels first works

Three specific reasons:

**1. Angels decide fast.** A committed angel takes 15-45 minutes across email + one call. A VC partnership takes 4-8 weeks minimum through 3-5 conversations. If you have limited runway, angels put dollars in the bank in days, not months.

**2. Angel names as warm-intro path to VCs.** When you email a VC saying "I've closed $400K from [Named Angel] and [Named Angel Two], now looking for a $1.5M lead" — the VC pattern-matches to social proof. The angels didn't have to make an intro. Their names on your cap table ARE the intro.

**3. Angels validate the story cheap.** Ten "no thanks" replies from angels teaches you which parts of the story need work — for a $25K exposure per angel. Ten "no thanks" from VCs teaches you the same thing but burns 12 weeks and 40% of your target VC list, since VCs share pipeline notes.

The angel round is basically pre-VC market research at a discount.

## Why "angels only" caps you

Angels commit small. $25-100K per angel is typical. Getting to $1.5M from angels alone means closing 15-30 angels. That's:

- Every angel is a separate conversation
- Every angel has a separate reference call to run
- Every angel wants an update every 3-6 months once they invest
- Cap table gets crowded fast
- Coordinating a "lead angel" who takes the pro-rata is possible but hard

Above $500-800K raised, angels-only starts to break down. You're spending 60% of your time on relationship management for 5% of the round. That's when you graduate to a VC-led round.

## Why "VCs only" hurts first-time founders

Skipping angels means walking into VC partner meetings with no external validation. The partner has to underwrite two things:

1. Is the founder plausible?
2. Is the business plausible?

If you have angel commitments, question 1 is answered for them. Five committed angels means five sophisticated people already believed the founder is plausible. Now the partner only has to underwrite question 2.

Without angels, the partner has to figure out both. That takes longer conversations, more diligence, more references. The process drags.

Repeat founders with a prior exit can sometimes skip angels — their name IS the validation. First-time founders shouldn't.

## The right sequence

Concrete plan:

**Weeks 1-4: Angel round**
- Target: $250-750K from 5-15 angels writing $25-100K each
- Focus on angels who bring more than money: relevant network, prior exit in your space, ability to make VC intros
- Structure: SAFEs at whatever cap you're going to price the VC round at (or 20-30% below)
- Signal to angels: "Building the round now, VC lead coming in phase 2"

**Weeks 5-14: VC round**
- Target: $1-2M from 1-2 VC leads writing $500K-1.5M each
- Reference the angel commitments in every intro email
- Warm intros from angels are your primary channel
- Structure: SAFE at your cap, MFN for the angels
- Signal to VCs: "Round is closing on [date], want you at the top of it"

**Weeks 15-18: Close**
- Consolidate remaining allocation with follow-on angels
- Sign SAFEs
- Wire funds
- Round closed

Total: 12-18 weeks, split $500-1000K from angels, $1-2M from VCs, on one cap.

## When to break the "angels first" rule

Some founders should skip angels:

**Repeat founders with prior exit.** Your name IS the validation. Angels don't add signal you don't already have. Straight to VCs is fine.

**Founders with unusual proprietary access.** Ex-Anthropic, ex-Stripe engineering, ex-Sequoia associate — you have direct paths to specific VCs who will move fast without needing external validation.

**Founders where speed is the entire game.** If a competitor is 4 weeks ahead of you and you need to close in 30 days flat, skip angels — you can't afford the sequence. Take the first credible VC term you can get.

**Founders raising deeply out-of-thesis rounds.** If you're raising $50K on a personal SAFE from a family member, you're not doing an angel round — that's a friends-and-family bridge. Different playbook.

For everyone else — first-time founders raising a real pre-seed or seed — angels first, VCs second.

## Who counts as an angel

The word "angel" gets used loosely. Categories:

- **Operator angels:** current or former operators (founders, execs, engineers) writing personal checks. Most valuable — they bring network + credibility + real feedback.
- **Financial angels:** wealthy individuals investing personally as an asset class. Less valuable per dollar — they don't move VCs by name.
- **Angel groups / syndicates:** organized groups (like AngelList Syndicates, HBS Alumni Angels, Golden Seeds). Efficient way to raise from multiple angels at once, but you cede some deal control to the syndicate lead.
- **Micro-VCs:** small funds ($5-25M AUM) writing $50-250K checks. Technically institutional but behave like angels — decide fast, less due diligence.

For the angel-first sequence, prioritize operator angels + micro-VCs. Financial angels are OK to close out the round but not the ones to lead with.

## Common mistakes

- **Taking dumb money.** Not every angel check is worth taking. Angels who bring nothing beyond money crowd your cap table and create investor-relations overhead. Filter for value-add.
- **Letting angels set the cap too low.** Angels sometimes ask for aggressive caps ("we get in cheap"). If you agree, you're marking down the VC round before it starts. Set the cap at what you'll defend to VCs.
- **Waiting for the "right" angel.** You lose 4-6 weeks fishing for a specific named angel while 20 others go un-asked. Pipeline density beats angel prestige.
- **Not planning for the VC round upfront.** Angels commit against a story they expect a VC to fund. If the VC round drags, angels get nervous. Have a plan for the VC round before opening the angel round.
- **Structuring the angel round to preclude a VC lead.** Some angel-only structures (uncapped SAFEs, weird side letters) confuse VCs and slow their diligence. Keep the angel round clean.

## What to skip

Founder-side myths:

- **"Angels are for hobby founders, VCs are for real founders."** Wrong. Every real seed round has angels + VCs.
- **"Just raise from tier-1 VCs and skip everyone else."** Tier-1 VCs sometimes prefer a round with angel signal, not without.
- **"Angels take too long to coordinate."** Angels are FASTER than VCs. 15 angels in 3 weeks is common. 3 VCs in 8 weeks is common.
- **"I'll close angels after the VC lead."** You can, but you lose the leverage of angel signal to attract the VC. Reverse the order.

## FAQ

### How many angels should I raise from?

Depends on total angel target. $250K = 5-10 angels. $500K = 10-15 angels. Above $800K, cap table gets crowded — start pushing toward micro-VCs or wait for the VC lead.

### Can I raise angels and VCs at the same time?

Yes, but keep the angels a step ahead. Angel commitments become your leverage in VC conversations. Simultaneous means you lose that.

### What if I only need $500K?

Angels alone is fine. Skip the VC round entirely. You're doing a bridge or friends-and-family, not a seed.

### Should the cap for angels differ from the VC cap?

Same cap. Set MFN in the angel SAFEs — if a VC later negotiates a better cap, angels get pulled up. This is the standard structure.

### What if a VC wants to lead before I've raised any angels?

Take the VC. If a real lead surfaces early, don't slow down for the theoretical angel round.

### What if an angel wants to lead the whole round?

Rare but possible. If an angel writes $500K+ and helps close others, you have a "super-angel-led" round. Uncommon but works.

### Should I use AngelList Rolling Fund or similar syndicate structures?

If it fits your check-writer, yes. Efficient way to consolidate multiple angels into one SAFE line. Watch for syndicate lead fees and side letter requirements.
