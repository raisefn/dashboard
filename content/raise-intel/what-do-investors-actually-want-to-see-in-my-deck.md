## VCs don't read your deck. They scan it.

You've been told the pitch deck is a strategic document. It isn't. It's a 90-second scan.

Every pre-seed VC gets 15-30 decks a week. They open your PDF, scroll, and decide within a minute whether to take the meeting. If the 4 slides that matter don't land in that minute, they don't read the rest. If the slides that matter land, they email you within an hour.

Most decks have 12-15 slides. Investors read 4 of them. The other 8 are noise. Some of the noise is worse than useless: it leaks information that gives investors reasons to pass.

## The 4 slides that actually matter

### Problem — specific, felt, ideally with a war story

Not "the fundraising process is broken." That's a Wikipedia intro. That's a topic, not a problem.

Give them the specific pain, felt from the inside. "I raised $30M for my last company. I took 1,128 meetings. My conversion rate was 2.1%. I was completely blind — I had no idea which investors were actually going to write a check and which ones were just taking meetings." That's a problem slide. Specific numbers. Personal war story. Earned authority.

If you didn't live the pain, the problem slide is where you lose them. If you did live it, this is your unfair advantage. Lead with it. Slide 1.

### Solution — why NOW is when this works

Not "we use AI." Every pitch says "we use AI" in 2026.

Why can this be built now that couldn't be built two years ago? What changed — model costs, infrastructure, distribution, buyer behavior, regulatory unlock? Name it. Be specific.

The solution slide is not "here's our product." It's "here's why our product works now, and why it wouldn't have worked before."

### Traction — specific numbers, not vanity

$10K in month one sounds great until an investor asks "what's your MRR?" and the honest answer is $0 because that $10K was one-time setup fees. If your answer differs from what you led with, you've lost credibility, and it's slide 4.

Reframe honestly. Instead of "$10K MRR month one" say "$10K revenue from 3 design partners at $1,999 setup. 14 founders on the platform, 11 actively raising through it right now. Those are the case studies that will drive self-serve." Investors respect the honest reframe far more than the inflated number.

Traction is your slope, not your point. Show the line, not the dot.

### Team — why YOU are the team that wins this

Not "we're a passionate group of builders." Every founder is that.

What have you specifically built before? What did it exit for? Why is your background the ONLY background that could build this specific thing right now? Solo technical founder with a prior exit and 5 years in the sector: that's a team slide. Solo technical founder with no track record: still a team slide, but the frame is "AI-native builder, shipping this product with AI as leverage." Own the story.

Solo is pitchable at pre-seed if the story is right. Buried is not.

## The slide 90% of AI pre-seed decks are missing

**Competitive.**

If you don't have a competitive slide and it's 2026, VCs assume you don't know your competitors, which means you assume you have no competition, which means you haven't done the market work.

Every AI pre-seed VC in 2026 is going to ask: "How are you different from [substitute your vertical's list — for fundraising AI, it's Rebank, Aumni, Carta's LP tools, Signal by NFX, Harmonic, Visible.vc, and every YC batch's fundraising CRM]?" If you don't name them and explain why you win, they assume you don't know they exist. The default assumption is that you're either uninformed or hiding.

Add the slide. Name the competitors. State your differentiation in one sentence. Be honest about which parts of the market they own and why you don't compete there.

The competitive slide is where a "we use AI" pitch either becomes a real thesis or gets passed on.

## The 8 slides that are usually bloat

Cut these unless there's a specific reason to keep them:

1. **The "trends" slide.** VCs know the trends better than you.
2. **The TAM slide.** "$425B market" — nobody believes the number and the bottoms-up math is more honest anyway.
3. **A standalone "why now" slide.** Bake it into problem or solution.
4. **The vision slide.** Nice-to-have. Cuttable if space is tight.
5. **The advisor slide.** Investors don't fund advisor lists.
6. **The 3-year projection.** Everyone knows it's fake.
7. **The full financial model.** Skip. Send it later if they ask.
8. **The "thank you" slide.** Why does this exist.

Every one of these was written by a founder who thought more slides equals more information equals more likely to raise. It doesn't. It signals "I don't know what to cut," which is a proxy for "I don't know what matters."

## The 8-slide sequence that works

1. **Hook** — your war story or the founder-market fit that gets them to slide 2
2. **Problem** — specific, felt, with the pain quantified
3. **Solution** — why now
4. **Traction** — honest numbers, slope not point
5. **Team** — why you win
6. **Competitive** — the slide that separates real theses from vibes
7. **Business model + use of funds** — how you make money, and how the $X you're raising becomes runway to what specific milestone
8. **Ask** — the round size, current commitments, and one clean CTA

Not 12 slides. Not 15. Eight. If you can't tell the story in 8, you don't have the story yet — send a Notion doc while you figure it out.

## The one thing to test before you send

Before you send the deck to any investor, do this: give it to a founder who has NOT been in your product for a month. Ask them to read it in 90 seconds and tell you back what your company does and why they should invest.

If they can't do it in 90 seconds, no VC will either. Rewrite until they can.
