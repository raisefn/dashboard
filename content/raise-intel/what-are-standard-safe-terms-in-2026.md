## "Standard" SAFE terms have drifted. Read every line.

The SAFE went from radically-simple in 2013 to increasingly-negotiable in 2026. Investors added clauses over 10 years. Some are reasonable. Some are terms that used to be reserved for priced rounds and shouldn't be in a SAFE. Founders who signed the "Y Combinator standard SAFE template" three years ago got a different document than founders signing "standard" SAFEs today.

The word "standard" has become misleading. What matters is what's in the specific document you're signing, not what a founder-guide-blog-post from 2020 says about "the SAFE."

Read every line. Understand every clause. Push back on the ones that don't belong. The ones below are the current negotiation zone as of mid-2026.

## Cap-only vs cap-with-discount

**Locked as default:** cap-only SAFEs. Discount clauses are effectively dead at pre-seed.

Why: cap alone gives investors the downside protection they care about (max price at conversion). Adding a discount was mostly a legacy from convertible notes and doubled the negotiation cost without clarifying anything. Modern sophisticated investors write cap-only.

If an investor asks for a discount alongside your cap, they're either inexperienced or trying to squeeze extra ownership without pricing it into the cap. Push back. "Standard is cap-only now."

## Post-money vs pre-money SAFE

**Standard:** post-money SAFE. Adopted by YC in 2018, widely used since.

- **Post-money SAFE:** dilution calculated after the SAFE converts. Cleaner for investors. Their ownership percentage is guaranteed at conversion.
- **Pre-money SAFE:** dilution calculated before the SAFE converts. Older. Ownership percentage floats based on subsequent rounds.

Post-money is standard for a reason — it's simpler and matches how priced rounds work. Pre-money SAFEs create ownership confusion at conversion and are largely obsolete.

If an investor pushes pre-money, they're usually trying to game the math. If you push pre-money to try to reduce your dilution, you're usually confused about how SAFEs actually stack.

Sign post-money. Move on.

## MFN clauses

**Locked as non-negotiable, always include:** MFN (Most Favored Nation).

An MFN clause says: if you extend better terms to a later investor in the same SAFE round, existing SAFEs automatically get those terms. Prevents you from screwing early investors by offering later ones sweetheart caps.

Every sophisticated investor demands MFN. If they don't ask for it, they're inexperienced. Every founder should include it — it's not against you; it just enforces fairness across the round.

Never negotiate MFN out. It's the one clause that's actually locked.

## Pro-rata rights

**Was standard, now negotiable.**

Pro-rata rights let an investor invest additional capital in future rounds to maintain their ownership percentage. In 2015-2020, pro-rata was default in most SAFEs. By 2023, most institutional investors kept it but individuals (angels) started losing it. In 2026, sophisticated founders don't automatically grant pro-rata to everyone.

**When to grant pro-rata:**
- Lead investor writing a meaningful check ($250K+) → yes, include
- Institutional pre-seed VC → yes, include
- Angel writing $25-100K → no, don't include
- Strategic investor with real value-add → yes, include with size limit
- Any investor who explicitly asks for it → discuss, decide case-by-case

**Why to be selective:** pro-rata rights fill your future rounds automatically. If you give it to 20 angels + 3 lead investors + 5 strategic partners, your next round is 40% pre-committed to prior investors before you've talked to a single new fund. That kills optionality.

Standard 2026 approach: pro-rata for lead + major investors only, cap at proportional check size, offer 30-day exercise window at next round.

## Information rights

**Was reserved for priced rounds, now creeps into SAFEs.**

Information rights obligate you to send financial updates + operating metrics on a scheduled cadence (usually quarterly). Standard in priced rounds. Started appearing in SAFEs around 2022.

**When to accept:**
- Lead investor with real experience → fine, they'll ignore the update anyway if you're doing well
- Institutional VC with formal reporting requirements → fine
- Individual angel with no operating experience → push back, "reserved for the priced round"

Information rights aren't dangerous but they're overhead. Every quarter you're formally on the hook to write updates. If you give them to 30 angels, that's overhead for zero value (angels don't act on the data).

Grant selectively.

## Participation rights

**Reserved for priced rounds. Do NOT accept in a SAFE.**

Participation rights (or "participation preferred") let an investor take their money out first at exit AND then also participate in the upside as if they were a common shareholder. Double-dipping.

This has no business appearing in a SAFE. If an investor asks for participation rights in a SAFE, that's a warning sign — they don't understand SAFEs or they're trying to sneak priced-round terms into a cheaper document.

Push back firmly: "Participation is priced-round territory. Not in the SAFE."

## Side letter clauses

**Emerging pattern, mostly unfriendly.**

Some investors want additional protections outside the SAFE itself, in a side letter. Common asks:

- **Board observer rights.** Push back at pre-seed. Board observers slow decisions and add reporting overhead. Reserved for priced rounds.
- **Anti-dilution protection.** Should NOT be in a SAFE. If an investor demands it, they want priced-round protections without paying the priced-round price.
- **Vesting acceleration triggers.** Reasonable for founder vesting to have double-trigger acceleration on acquisition. Push back if they demand single-trigger or if they're demanding it apply to founders they don't lead-invest in.
- **Redemption rights.** No. Investors can't demand their money back on a SAFE. If they insist, walk.

Side letters are becoming the venue for terms investors know don't belong in the main SAFE. Read every side letter as carefully as the SAFE itself.

## What you should always include

- **MFN clause** (see above)
- **Standard "no fiduciary duty" language** for the investor as a SAFE holder (protects you from investors trying to sue as if they were shareholders)
- **Standard "not a security" acknowledgment** (regulatory hygiene)
- **Assignability restrictions** — investors can't sell/transfer the SAFE without your consent

## What you should push back on

- Discount alongside cap (dead, cap-only)
- Pre-money SAFE (obsolete)
- Pro-rata for small check-writers (grant selectively)
- Information rights for angels writing <$100K (unnecessary overhead)
- Participation rights in a SAFE (priced-round only)
- Board observer at pre-seed (priced-round only)
- Anti-dilution protection (priced-round only)
- Redemption rights (never)
- Single-trigger vesting acceleration on acquisition (only if investor is lead)

## What to always negotiate hard

- **Cap number itself.** See [What valuation should I put on my company at pre-seed](/raise-intel/what-valuation-should-i-put-on-my-company-at-pre-seed).
- **Pro-rata size limits.** If you grant pro-rata, cap the exercise size at their proportional check.
- **Info rights cadence.** Quarterly beats monthly. Semi-annual is even better.
- **Vesting terms for founders.** Standard is 4-year with 1-year cliff. Push for double-trigger acceleration.
- **Amendments language.** SAFE amendments should require majority-in-interest of SAFE holders (not each individual), so you're not stuck on one investor blocking a modification.

## Legal review isn't optional

A "standard" SAFE from YC template is a good starting point. But 90% of SAFEs founders sign have been modified from that template. Every modification is a potential landmine.

Have a startup lawyer review every SAFE before signing. Not a general lawyer — a startup-specialist who reviews SAFEs weekly. Cost is $500-1500 per review. Way cheaper than fixing a bad clause at conversion.

Don't rely on "the investor's lawyer sent me a standard doc." Their lawyer optimizes for them, not you.

## What to skip

Founder-side myths that waste time:

- **"Convertible notes are cleaner than SAFEs."** Convertible notes have accrued interest + maturity dates + repayment obligations. SAFEs don't. SAFEs are cleaner.
- **"Priced rounds under $2M make sense."** Priced rounds add $10-20K in legal fees + board setup + cap table complexity. Rarely worth it under $2M.
- **"Uncapped SAFEs work at pre-seed."** Sophisticated investors won't touch uncapped SAFEs. Amateur-signal.
- **"MFN can be dropped for the right investor."** No. It's non-negotiable in every direction.

## FAQ

### Can I use a SAFE for amounts over $2M?

Yes, technically. Practically, at $2M+ you're at the edge where sophisticated investors want a priced round. If you're raising $3M+, expect institutional VCs to push for priced.

### What's the difference between MFN and pro-rata?

MFN pulls existing investors UP to any better terms you offer later. Pro-rata gives investors the right to INVEST MORE in your future rounds to maintain ownership. Different clauses, different purposes.

### Should I include a "conversion trigger" beyond next equity round?

Standard SAFEs convert at next equity financing OR liquidity event OR dissolution. That's fine. Don't add custom triggers.

### What about "successor liability" clauses?

Standard SAFE says obligations pass to acquirer. That's fine, don't modify.

### Should the SAFE include a "no shop" clause?

No. SAFEs shouldn't have exclusivity clauses. If an investor demands one, that's priced-round behavior in a SAFE.

### Do I need a lawyer to write my SAFE?

Use the YC standard template as your starting point. Have a lawyer review before signing. Don't rely on the investor's lawyer.
