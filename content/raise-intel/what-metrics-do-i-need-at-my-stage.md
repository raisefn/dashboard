## Slope beats level. Show the line, not the point.

Investors don't fund revenue. They fund revenue trajectory. Five thousand MRR growing 30% month-over-month for six months is more fundable than $30K MRR that hasn't moved in three months. The number that matters is the derivative, not the value.

Founders get this wrong constantly. They lead with "we have $50K MRR" and skip the growth rate. Or they show "growing 200% MoM" without noting the base is $500. Neither works. Investors read both level and slope and only fund when both look right for the stage.

The specific metrics that matter change by stage. Show the wrong metric and you look premature or thin. Match the stage.

## Pre-seed: credibility signals, not revenue

At pre-seed, most companies don't have revenue. That's expected. What investors actually underwrite:

- **Team credibility.** Founders' prior experience, prior exits, unfair advantages. If you're a repeat founder in the space, that's the metric. If you're first-time, "why you specifically" needs a defensible answer.
- **Wedge specificity.** What is the ONE thing you do first, in one sentence, that opens the bigger market? Vague wedges get passed. Sharp wedges get first meetings.
- **Market pull signal.** Not "the market is $X billion." Signals: 3 design partners signed LOIs. 10 warm inbound customer requests before you started outreach. Waitlist of 500 with $50K in pre-committed deposits. Something concrete that shows real demand.
- **Product velocity.** How fast do you ship? "MVP in 6 weeks, first paying customer in 8" is a stronger signal than "we're building an AI platform."

What's optional at pre-seed:

- MRR (nice to have if you have it, not required)
- Growth rate (nice to have, small base makes it noisy anyway)
- Customer retention (impossible to measure at this stage)
- Unit economics (unclear when the model isn't proven)

Skip forcing metrics that don't exist. Faking pre-seed metrics reads as amateur.

## Seed: MRR + growth + retention

At seed ($1-5M raise), investors expect real product-market fit signal. Specific numbers:

- **$5-25K MRR** is the range. Below $5K = you're early seed (closer to pre-seed extension). $25K+ MRR moves you toward Series A conversations.
- **15-25% MoM growth** sustained for 3-4 months. Below 15% = investors question the market pull. Above 25% consistently = strong signal, but watch for base-effect noise.
- **Net revenue retention (NRR) above 90%** for SaaS. Above 100% signals real expansion motion. Below 80% = churn problem, hard to raise.
- **D30 retention above 20% for consumer** (higher for prosumer, 40%+). Below 20% = product isn't sticky yet.
- **Sales cycle < 30 days for SMB SaaS**. Longer = extended process required to hit growth targets.
- **CAC payback under 12 months.** LTV/CAC > 3x. Both hard to measure precisely at seed, but rough signal matters.

The stack matters. $15K MRR growing 20% MoM with 105% NRR reads completely differently than $15K MRR growing 20% MoM with 75% NRR. First one gets funded. Second one triggers "let's revisit in 6 months" passes.

## Series A: cohort quality + unit economics + market pull

At Series A ($5-15M raise), the entire fund model has to work. Metrics:

- **$80-150K MRR minimum.** Below $80K = you're extended seed. Above $150K = mid-A / late-A.
- **Growth rate: 10-20% MoM sustained.** Series A growth rate is lower than seed by design (bigger base) but has to be consistent. Erratic growth = not fundable.
- **NRR above 105% for SaaS.** Series A investors underwrite expansion. Below 100% NRR = deal breaker for most.
- **D30 retention: 40%+ for consumer. D30 revenue retention: 60%+ for SMB SaaS.**
- **Cohort quality trend.** Newer cohorts have to be as good as or better than older cohorts. If cohort quality is declining, growth is unhealthy.
- **CAC payback under 18 months. LTV/CAC > 4x.** Both measured with real data, not projections.
- **Market pull evidence.** Inbound + referral > 30% of new customers = real market pull. Below 30% = growth is bought, not pulled.

Series A investors dig deep on these. Don't expect to raise a Series A with vague metrics.

## The framing that works: show the line

Regardless of stage, present metrics as trajectories:

- **Weak framing:** "$15K MRR"
- **Strong framing:** "$5K MRR in January, $8K in February, $12K in March, $15K in April — 25% MoM sustained, 105% NRR, all inbound"

Weak framing shows a point. Strong framing shows a line + confidence + market pull signal. Same MRR, wildly different narrative.

Include:
- 6-month time series if you have it
- Growth rate as a rolling average, not just current month
- Retention curves as cohort views, not aggregate
- Any pull signal (inbound, referral, waitlist, LOIs)

## Common metric mistakes

- **Leading with TAM/SAM/SOM.** Investors don't believe them. Skip or bury. Lead with actual traction.
- **"$0 MRR but massive potential."** Frame it honestly: pre-revenue, here's what's real (design partners, LOIs, product velocity).
- **Cumulative revenue instead of MRR.** MRR is the metric that matters. Cumulative is theater.
- **Ignoring retention.** Growth without retention = leaky bucket. Investors know this. Show retention early.
- **Vanity metrics.** Users, downloads, signups — only useful if they translate to revenue or engagement. "50,000 users" without revenue or engagement data = ignored.
- **Comparing your metric to industry averages.** Investors know the averages. They fund founders whose metrics beat averages, not match them.

## What to skip

Metrics that founders overshare:

- **"We built 47 features."** Feature count is not a metric.
- **"Our team has 200 years of combined experience."** Nobody cares.
- **"Our press coverage includes TechCrunch."** Press ≠ traction.
- **"NPS of 72."** Fine to include but rarely load-bearing.
- **"Twitter followers."** Not a metric unless you're a consumer-social company where distribution is the moat.

## The metric that matters most at your stage

If forced to pick ONE metric per stage:

- **Pre-seed:** wedge specificity + team credibility (not a number, but the signal that determines the round)
- **Seed:** growth rate at a real MRR base
- **Series A:** cohort quality (are newer cohorts as good as older ones?)
- **Series B+:** unit economics + net dollar retention

Focus your pitch on the ONE metric that answers "is this business getting healthier over time?" That's what investors are actually solving for.

## FAQ

### What if I don't have any revenue at pre-seed?

Fine. Focus on team, wedge, market pull signal. Don't fake revenue. Investors expect pre-revenue at pre-seed.

### What if my growth rate is inconsistent?

Show the underlying reason. Product launch spike + normalization is fine. Erratic without explanation = investor pause. Explain the noise.

### Do I need cohort retention curves at seed?

Nice to have. If you have them, show them. If not, aggregate retention is fine.

### What's the minimum MRR for a seed round?

$5K if the growth rate is exceptional (30%+ MoM). $15K+ for typical seed. Below $5K = you're in extended pre-seed / early seed territory.

### How do I handle metrics that are trending down?

Address it head-on. "Growth slowed in Q3 because [specific reason]. Here's what we're doing to fix it." Investors respect honesty. Hiding downtrends erodes trust when they eventually notice.

### What if my metrics don't map cleanly to SaaS benchmarks?

Explain your unit of measurement. Marketplace businesses use GMV + take rate. Consumer subscription uses D30 + LTV. Deep tech uses milestones + pilot revenue. Pick the right frame for your business shape.

### Is NPS a real metric?

Yes but rarely load-bearing. Include if it's exceptional (70+). Skip if it's average.
